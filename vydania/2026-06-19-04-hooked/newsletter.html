A quick word

This one is personal.

No project update, no vendor takedown, no architecture diagram. Just an honest look at what happens when the tool that makes you impossibly fast also makes you unable to stop.
 
01 The first hit

Last summer I described something to Claude Code that I'd been putting off for weeks. It built it in minutes. Not "started it" - done. Working.

I stayed up until 2 AM. Not because I had to - because I couldn't stop. Every finished thing unlocked three new ideas. Every idea was "just 10 more minutes."

The next morning I wasn't tired. I was planning what to build next.
02 The loop

The speed is the drug. The gap between "I want this" and "I have this" shrank to almost nothing - and my brain couldn't get enough.

I'd sit down after dinner "just to try one thing." Click - it's 2 AM. Weekends, holidays - I had Claude open on all of them.

It works like a slot machine. Sometimes the AI nails it first try, sometimes it takes three attempts - but the win always comes fast. And here's the trap: when you build software at midnight, nobody tells you to stop. "I'm being productive." "This is literally my job." "One more thing and then I'll stop."

For months I told myself it was discipline. It wasn't. It was the loop.
03 I'm not alone

I thought it was just me. Then I started reading.

64% of vibe coders report a "dopamine hit" from fast prototyping. Armin Ronacher - creator of Flask - spent two months building tools he never used. "The building itself became the point." One Reddit user: "It's been four months, and it's consuming me."

ScienceDirect proposed a formal diagnosis: Generative AI Addiction Syndrome. Behavioural patterns matching gambling and gaming. This isn't a metaphor anymore.

04 Where I am now

It's calmed down. Not because the tool got worse - because I adapted. The novelty faded enough for the rational brain to catch up.

But I still feel the pull. And I know it'll spike again with every model upgrade. The honest truth: this is the most productive I've ever been. It's also the closest I've come to something I'd genuinely call addictive behaviour around work. Those two things are the same thing.

◆  Tomi's Window A recurring pick

Each issue, one thing from the tech world that's caught my eye.

harnext   harnext.dev

Open-source AI coding agent with a Context Engine - it continuously indexes your Slack, GitHub, Jira, and other tools, then feeds only the relevant context to the agent. The result: 89% fewer tokens per query because it knows what matters before you ask.

● Runs locally, works with Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, Ollama
● Pulls live context from where your team actually works and talks
● This is where it's heading - AI agents that understand your org, not just your code

I believe tools like this will be standard soon. The missing piece for AI agents isn't smarter models - it's context transport: getting the right information from where your team lives (Slack, Jira, docs) into the agent's context window. It's early and small - just launched, nothing production-ready yet. But the direction is interesting. Maybe more on this in a future Floor 9.

If you've felt this too - the 2 AM sessions, the "just one more prompt," the inability to explain to your family why you're smiling at a terminal - you're not broken. You're just running a brain that evolved for scarcity on a tool built for abundance. The trick is to notice it.

Biggest credit goes to the team. I'm just the one writing it up.
