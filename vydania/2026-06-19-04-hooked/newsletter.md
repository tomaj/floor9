## Hook / a quick word
**This one is personal.**

No project update, no vendor takedown, no architecture diagram. Just an honest look at what happens when the tool that makes you impossibly fast also makes you unable to stop.

## 01 - The first hit
I remember the moment. Last autumn, I asked Claude to build something I'd been putting off for weeks. It took 4 minutes. Not "started in 4 minutes" - done. Working. Deployed.

And then I thought: what else? I stayed up until 2 AM. Not because I had to - because I couldn't stop. Every finished thing unlocked three new ideas. Every idea was "just 10 more minutes." The feedback loop was instant: think it, say it, see it, ship it. No friction. No waiting. No reason to stop.

The next morning I wasn't tired. I was already planning what to build next.

## 02 - Why you can't stop
Here's what nobody warns you about: **the speed is the drug.**

When you go from idea to working software in minutes, your brain rewires. The gap between "I want" and "I have" shrinks to almost nothing. That's not a productivity story - that's a dopamine story. Psychologists call it a **variable-ratio reinforcement schedule** - the same mechanism behind slot machines. Sometimes the AI nails it on the first try, sometimes it takes three attempts - but the hit always comes, and it always comes fast.

Neuroscience research shows that dopamine neurons fire most intensely when reward probability is uncertain - **peak activation at 50% reward probability** (Fiorillo, Tobler & Schultz, 2003). That's exactly what a prompt-and-run loop gives you. Not always perfect, not always broken - just unpredictable enough to keep you pulling the lever.

The difference from a slot machine? **The reward here is real.** You actually ship something. Which makes it worse - because you can justify it. "I'm being productive." "This is literally my job." "One more thing and then I'll stop."

Andrej Karpathy called it **"vibe coding"** in February 2025 - and it became Collins Dictionary's Word of the Year. An AI-first workflow where the machine handles the tedious, mechanical parts and you float in the exciting space of ideas and architecture. It sounds great until you realise you've been floating for 14 hours straight.

## 03 - We're not alone
I thought it was just me being obsessive. Then I started reading. Turns out this is everywhere.

A research paper (*Vibe Coding in Practice*, arXiv 2025) found that **64% of practitioners** described the process as producing a "dopamine hit" when prototypes came together quickly. One developer on r/vibecoding wrote: **"It's been four months, and it's consuming me. I can't stay away."** Another built 27 projects in that time - shipped 4 of them, spent $500+ on API costs, and reported 16-hour coding marathons.

Armin Ronacher - the creator of Flask - admitted he spent two months "excessively prompting" Claude, building tools he ultimately never used. **"The building itself became the point."**

A developer named Sanford described the feedback loop as nearly impossible to stop - and developed atrial fibrillation from the stress. Andrew Filev, CEO of an AI coding company, found himself debugging code for two hours while his family waited at the beach, realising he was gambling, not working.

The science backs it up. ScienceDirect published a paper in March 2025 proposing a formal diagnosis: **Generative AI Addiction Syndrome** - with behavioural addiction characteristics matching gambling and gaming. The patterns fit: inability to stop despite wanting to, escalation over time, continued engagement despite negative consequences.

## 04 - The slot machine in your terminal
Here's what makes it worse than doomscrolling: **it feels virtuous.**

Scrolling Twitter at 1 AM, you know you're wasting time. But building software at 1 AM? That feels like discipline. Like ambition. Like getting ahead. The compulsive creation hides behind the mask of productivity - which makes it harder to recognise and even harder to stop.

Developer Roman Hoffmann calls it the **"mental prompt carousel"** - even after you close the laptop, your brain keeps iterating. Generating hypothetical prompts, imagining solutions, running the loop offline.

One developer described it perfectly: **"Consistent reliability makes something a tool. Intermittent reliability makes it a slot machine."** And the near-miss is the worst part. Code that almost compiles, architecture that's 90% right - failure gives you permission to walk away, but a near-miss whispers that the next attempt will land.

## 05 - Where we are now
It's calmed down. Not because the tool got worse - because we adapted. The novelty faded enough for the rational brain to catch up.

But I still feel the pull. Open the terminal, type a prompt, watch the magic. It takes real effort to close the laptop and go for a walk. And I know it'll spike again with every major model upgrade - because each one makes the loop tighter and the reward bigger.

**The honest truth:** this is the most productive I've ever been. It's also the closest I've come to something I'd genuinely call addictive behaviour around work. Those two things aren't contradictions - they're the same thing.

## Kept honest
- I glamorised it for months. "Look how much I'm shipping!" - while burning through evenings and weekends at a pace that doesn't hold up.
- Speed makes you skip the thinking. Some things I shipped fast, I later had to rebuild - because I never stopped to ask whether they were right, only whether they worked. As one developer put it: "Working code feels like correct code. But those are not the same thing."
- There's a real skill degradation risk. When the AI handles everything, your brain stops retaining the details. You become an architect who forgot how to lay bricks.
- What I'd do differently: set boundaries earlier. Not "I'll stop when I'm done" - because you're never done. A timer. A hard stop. Walk away. The terminal has no natural stopping point - hitting Enter requires a keystroke, while closing requires a decision.
